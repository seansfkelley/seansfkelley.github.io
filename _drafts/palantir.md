Palantir gets a lot of deserved flak for its involvement with certain government agencies. The company generally and the CEO specifically have made no secret of their hawkishness and near-unconditional support for whatever the federal government does. Indeed a book written by the CEO (link) rather hamfistedly made this argument after Trump's second election, which had poor optics at the time and has only gotten worse after we decapitated Venezuela's (admittedly illegitimate) government unprompted and started a war with Iran.

But! I think people at large both misunderstand and underestimate Palantir's role.

The misunderstanding: data access. To my knowledge they do _not_ have access to the actual data beyond that intentionally made accessible to its employees by their customers for the purposes of training said customers on the product. (They offer hosted and on-premise versions; presumably there are access controls for the hosted version.) Point being, it is only the agencies/companies analyzing _their own_ data using Palantir's tools.

The underestimation: data analysis at large scale. The discussion seems to be largely about the existence of sensitive data. The problem goes deeper: Palantir's main use is data connectivity rather than harvesting; where before the laborious process of working through the bureaucracy to manually correlate disparate data sources and gain a fuller image of a suspect (say) helped keep abuse in check, Palantir enables notionally one-click surveillance, _all with data the government already had_.

In my view, Palantir is merely a vehicle -- though admittedly an enthusiastically participatory one -- for harder problems with different solutions that have an air of inevitability about them:

- Why does the government have all this sensitive data on so many people? Why is it legal to, say, collect ALPR data with minimal oversight, "just in case"? Where are the access controls? Can we reintroduce friction on purpose, such as with warrants?
- Philosophically, at which point does scale and connectivity become a problem in itself? Echoing my own [post on social media]({% post_url 2025-11-20-section-230 %}): yes, people always said shitty things about each other, but quantity has a quality all its own. Paper hate mail is not the same as swatting and doxxing.
